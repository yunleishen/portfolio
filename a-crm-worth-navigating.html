A CRM Worth Navigating

Sugar CRM is the internal tool VBG employees rely on to manage veterans, process claims, and review documents. It was built without a designer, and over time its tangled structure quietly cut claim submissions nearly in half. I rebuilt the information architecture around how each role actually works. Within a month, submissions doubled.

DIAGNOSIS

What was actually broken

Every role at VBG lives in Sugar: managing veterans and business clients, processing claims, reviewing documents, collaborating across teams. But the system had grown without design input, and it showed — in productivity, in morale, and eventually in revenue.

To see the damage precisely, I ran user interviews and field studies with representatives from each role, observing real workflows rather than asking about ideal ones. Three structural problems surfaced:

1

One navigation for everyone. The bar was crowded with links most roles never used, nested pages had no secondary navigation, and features like Notes and Activity appeared in different places on different pages — sometimes more than once.

2

Everything was deep. Key information sat 3–5 clicks away, related content was scattered across pages, and critical actions like file upload had no consistent home.

3

Key details hidden in plain sight. Claims, documents, and disability pages omitted basics like file names or the associated veteran; sorting followed no logic users recognized; upload buttons were small and buried.

REDESIGN

Rebuilding the map before the rooms

Waypoint 01 — Chart the terrain

Let users draw the architecture, card sorting across roles

I mapped every page and feature onto a virtual card-sorting board in Figma, then asked participants from each role to group, prioritize, and organize them around their actual workflows. Roles organized differently — by task type or by frequency — but clear shared patterns emerged for what needed to surface.

The new IA came directly from those mental models: categories reorganized around real workflows instead of arbitrary menus, missing-but-expected features added, and every key page reachable in one or two clicks.

Waypoint 02 — Redraw the main roads

Rebuilding the top level

The new navigation shows only the most frequently used links, with everything else in a “More” dropdown. The homepage was rearranged by task priority: low-usage features removed, replaced with work progress and key updates, plus quick links to each role’s top three pages and a preview of the latest claims, so the day starts on the homepage, not three menus deep.

Waypoint 03 — Fit each room to its use

Match the layout to the data, not the template

Not every page deserved the same treatment. Claims and Disabilities — where users act on individual records — became card-based layouts with key information on the card face, cutting unnecessary clicks. Accounts and Documents — large datasets users scan — kept table views, but gained the identifying columns that had been missing.

I also simplified task-focused forms with role leaders: unnecessary fields removed, required fields clearly labeled per task. And upload — the action users hunted for most — got a universal button on the Documents page, consistent everywhere.

Reflection

When several roles share one system, information architecture is the product. Every role here had distinct tasks in the same tool — and a poor IA taxed all of them equally. A strong one shows each person what matters to them, cuts cognitive load, and makes the same system feel purpose-built for everyone.
