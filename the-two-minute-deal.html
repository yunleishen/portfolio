BACKGROUND

The marketplace nobody built for the small players

Influencer marketing platforms serve reputable brands and top-tier creators. Nano-influencers and small local businesses — arguably the best-matched pair, given their shared local focus and tight budgets — were left to find each other through DMs and cold outreach. Our goal: bridge that gap with a B2C mobile app, and ship the MVP in three months.

RESEARCH

Six weeks lost to negotiation and reporting

Interviews, surveys, and competitive analysis sharpened the problem. Over half of nano-influencers hunted for sponsorships through social platforms and direct outreach. Small businesses couldn’t find cost-effective creators. And both sides bled time in the same place: pre-campaign negotiation ran up to five weeks of repeated introductions and haggling, and post-campaign reporting ate another week because nobody could afford professional tracking tools.

A journey map of the influencer’s current experience pointed at three phases worth attacking: negotiation, content creation, and measurement. That became the design brief — streamline the collaboration itself.

HOW I DESIGNED IT — ZERO TO ONE

None of it arrived fully formed

PHASE 01 — FRAME

Frame the problem as design questions

I reframed each journey-map pain point as a design question — how might a deal start without a conversation? how might content prove itself without a report? — giving every later concept one brief to answer: streamline the collaboration itself.

PHASE 02 — IDEATE

Sketch the mechanism before the screens

Brainstorming with the PM, pressure-testing with engineering, and validating with potential users happened on paper first. Rapid sketches explored how a deal could start, run, and close — and converged on the structural moves: tier-based contracts to kill negotiation, and post-campaign Tasks to kill the report. Sketching the mechanism, not the UI, is what let us throw away weak ideas cheaply.

PHASE 03 — STRUCTURE

Wireframe the architecture and test it

Low-fidelity wireframes put the concepts in users’ hands: the single Business page, the top tab bar, the Details-then-Tasks sequence. Testing at this fidelity settled the arguments quickly — where tiers should live, when the page should hand over to Tasks — before any pixels were precious.

PHASE 04 — VISUALIZE

Make the concept legible at high fidelity

High fidelity was where the concepts became self-explanatory: tiers rendered as familiar pricing-style cards so the choice reads at a glance, Tasks as a step-by-step checklist with maps and upload-success states so closing a deal feels like finishing a to-do list. The visual language did the onboarding — no tutorial needed for the MVP.

Reflection

Shipping an MVP in three months meant choosing imperfect answers on purpose — and saying so. “Likes” aren’t a real business metric, but a legible, launchable one beat a rigorous one we couldn’t ship. The growth numbers suggest the trade was right.
