The End of the Paper Trail

Veteran Benefits Guide helps veterans file disability claims with the VA. The work ran on paper — and paper couldn't keep up with demand. I led the design of Valor, the platform that brought the entire claim online, cutting processing time by nearly half and freeing veterans from leaning on staff at every turn.

DISCOVERY

Where the process broke down

VBG’s paper heavy claims process only worked because staff carried every step by hand, and it couldn’t keep up with demand. As the first in-house designer, I interviewed every operations role that touches a claim. Four frictions shaped everything that followed:

01documents

Days lost to paperwork

Requests for more documents after submission restarted the wait.

02forms

Disability Benefits Questionnaires too confusing to finish alone

Veterans leaned on case managers and revised them repeatedly.

03scheduling

No control over evaluations

Every scheduling change routed through a coordination team.

04status

Zero visibility into claim status

The only option was calling customer service.

GOING DIGITAL

Four moves, one claim

Move 01 — Straighten the path

Compress the journey to nine legible steps

I mapped the full journey and found redundant steps, information collected in fragments, and heavy staff dependence. The digital process consolidates everything into nine steps — from sign-up through decision letter and invoice — with information collected once, up front.

Move 02 — Build the spine

A dashboard framework that scales with the process

Nine steps with many tasks each needed a predictable structure. I explored two directions and rejected both: an all-tasks checklist dashboard gave a full overview but overwhelmed users and let them attempt steps out of order; a pure stepper with progressive disclosure kept focus but hid the big picture.

The final framework combines them: a high-level stepper for orientation, with a vertical task checklist scoped to the current step. Veterans always see where they are in the whole, but only work on what’s in front of them — and can’t skip anything critical.

Move 03 — Retire the form

Turn the Disability Benefits Questionnaire from a form into a conversation

Disability Benefits Questionnaires vary by disability, so I worked with our medical consultant to find the pattern beneath them: four recurring question types — factual, multiple-choice, dropdown, and conditional follow-ups. That insight let me design a small set of reusable question cards, each with clear states, that any Disability Benefits Questionnaire can be assembled from.

Where language failed, visuals took over. For range-of-motion questions — “how far can you bend?” — I added illustrated references, with the full text explanation tucked behind a reference-chart button. The form stayed clean; the detail stayed reachable.

Move 04 — Close the loops

Make documents and scheduling self-correcting

To end the document back-and-forth, I replaced the bare upload button with document cards carrying status — Uploaded, Kickback, Accepted — a requirements checklist that catches wrong file types and expired documents before they cause delays, and multi-file upload.

Scheduling was the harder judgment call. I wanted veterans to choose network, clinic, and examiner like any healthcare booking — but business partnerships meant we couldn’t reveal names or exact addresses, or veterans might book around the system. The answer was a privacy-preserving map: simplified terrain, major roads only, general area shown. An appointment books as “Pending” with location “TBD”, and the full address is revealed once the network confirms. Veterans got confidence and control; the business kept its constraints intact.

And underneath it all

A design system from zero

None of this existed before, so I built the design system alongside the product — cards, inputs, buttons, and status patterns aligned to VBG’s brand — so every later screen shipped faster and looked like it belonged.

Looking back

The hardest design problem here wasn’t the interface — it was deciding how much to show. Balancing veterans’ need for transparency against real business constraints taught me that trust can be designed with less information, not more, as long as what you do show is honest and timely.
